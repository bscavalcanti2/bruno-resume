Me

Business, Technology & AI.

Growing up in Goiânia, Bruno's heroes were Carl Sagan and Einstein — real, flawed, brilliant humans who refused to accept the limits of their circumstances. With no observatory or science club in sight, he taught himself astronomy from used magazines and old library books, dreaming of becoming an astrophysicist one day.

The path to the stars went through an unexpected detour: Computer Engineering at USP. What started as a strategy to reach astronomy became its own obsession. He fell in love with technology, and for the next decade built everything — infrastructure, ERPs, databases, BI systems, Python automation. He became a senior engineer the hard way, from the ground up.

"I thought I had traded my telescope for a keyboard. The universe had other plans."

In 2020, amid a global pandemic, Sergio Sacani — one of Brazil's most prominent science communicators — offered a remote astronomy course. Bruno didn't hesitate. Inside those lectures, between orbital mechanics and stellar physics, he found something unexpected: Big Data. Kafka. Python for data. Real tools built to process the vast, unstructured chaos of the cosmos.

The realization hit like a supernova: he had built the skills to find water in the dust of Mars. In just two years, he rewound an entire career — from Data Analyst to Data Scientist to Data Engineer — mastering it all with the relentless curiosity of someone who once read Carl Sagan by flashlight.

Then came the architectural insight that would define his leadership: the hardest problems in data aren't technical — they're architectural. Trade-offs that were invisible to most engineers were obvious to him. He could see the ADRs before they were written. He moved from building systems to designing them, and from designing them to leading the people who build them.

Today at Syngenta, Bruno leads a global team of 8 engineers building something that has never quite existed before: a data platform designed and operated entirely by AI agents, where non-technical stakeholders anywhere in the world can create pipelines using natural language. While global leaders at companies like Coca-Cola and Walmart struggle to keep pace with AI transformation, Bruno sees it as the natural continuation of the journey that started with a boy and a used astronomy magazine in Goiânia.
